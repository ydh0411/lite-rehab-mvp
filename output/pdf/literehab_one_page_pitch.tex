{\bfseries\color{ReportNavy}\fontsize{29}{30.5}\selectfont LiteRehab Fusion}\par
\vspace{1.2mm}
{\color{ReportMuted}\fontsize{11.5}{13.2}\selectfont
Real-time feedback for upper-limb rehabilitation practice at home.}\par

\vspace{6.0mm}
\sectionhead{Summary}
LiteRehab Fusion is a coursework engineering prototype that combines a
forearm-worn motion sensor with camera-based posture estimation. The system
recognises the demonstrated exercise, counts repetitions, checks selected
movement features, and records synchronized motion and posture data. It is
designed to support home practice under professional guidance; it does not
make treatment decisions or assess clinical recovery.

\vspace{6.0mm}

\begin{minipage}[t]{0.477\linewidth}
  \vspace{0pt}\raggedright

  \sectionhead{Background}
  Home-exercise programmes ask patients to practise away from the clinic.
  During these sessions, a patient may not notice that a movement is too fast,
  that the range is limited, or that the trunk is compensating. The
  physiotherapist usually reviews progress at a later appointment rather than
  observing each repetition.

  \vspace{5.2mm}
  \sectionhead{Aim}
  The project examines whether wearable motion sensing and a camera view of
  posture can provide immediate, understandable feedback during a prescribed
  upper-limb exercise. A synchronized session record is retained for possible
  later review.

  \vspace{5.2mm}
  \sectionhead{System design}

  \subhead{Wearable unit}
  \vspace{0.8mm}
  An MPU6050 samples forearm motion at 50 Hz. An ESP32 wearable transmits the
  samples over BLE to an ESP32-S3 receiver, which maintains the local exercise
  state and repetition count.

  \vspace{3.5mm}
  \subhead{Posture input}
  \vspace{0.8mm}
  A MaixCAM2 video stream supplies a wider view of the movement. Computer-based
  posture estimation is used to check trunk position alongside the forearm
  signal.

  \vspace{3.5mm}
  \subhead{Analysis and interface}
  \vspace{0.8mm}
  The dashboard aligns the motion and posture streams, displays the current
  status, and stores a synchronized session record. A classroom CNN-BiGRU
  baseline is available with a rule-based fallback.

\end{minipage}
\hfill
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.477\linewidth}
  \vspace{0pt}\raggedright

  \sectionhead{Prototype capabilities}
  The current MVP demonstrates:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item elbow-flexion and forearm-rotation exercise recognition;
    \item repetition counting from the wearable motion signal;
    \item checks for excessive speed and insufficient movement range;
    \item a posture check for trunk compensation;
    \item local status feedback and synchronized IMU and pose logging.
  \end{itemize}

  \vspace{5.0mm}
  \sectionhead{Intended use}

  \subhead{During home practice}
  \vspace{0.8mm}
  The person receives feedback while performing the exercise, leaving time to
  adjust a later repetition in the same session.

  \vspace{3.5mm}
  \subhead{Between appointments}
  \vspace{0.8mm}
  A physiotherapist could review the session record when discussing home
  practice. Exercise selection and all clinical decisions remain with the
  physiotherapist.

  \vspace{5.2mm}
  \sectionhead{Limitations and next steps}
  The exercise set is limited, and the classroom model was trained on a small
  public dataset. The prototype has not been clinically validated and makes no
  claim about diagnostic accuracy or patient outcomes.

  \vspace{2.3mm}
  The next step is supervised usability testing with physiotherapists and
  representative users. This work would examine the clarity and timing of the
  feedback and guide the collection of more representative movement data.
